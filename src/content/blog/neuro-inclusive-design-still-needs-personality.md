There's a version of _"accessible design"_ that quietly turns into _"design with all the character sanded off."_ Big neutral cards, safe grey text, zero motion, zero opinion. I get where it comes from. When you're designing for people who get overwhelmed easily, the instinct is to strip everything down until there's nothing left to overwhelm them with. But calm and characterless aren't the same thing, and I think treating them as synonyms actually does neurodivergent users a disservice. It assumes we want to disappear into a beige interface, when really we just want the noise turned down.

## What I actually mean by "calm"

When I design something low-stimulation, I'm usually working with 3 things at once, not one.

- **Clarity:** clear structure, readable text, enough space to actually focus on what matters – this is the part everyone gets right, because it's the easiest to check off a list

- **Personality:** warmth, expression, something that still feels like a person made it. A colour palette with some life in it. Illustrations instead of stock icons. A voice in the copy that sounds like someone talking to you, not a terms-and-conditions page

- **Reduced motion, not zero motion:** less movement, more comfort, but with the option to adjust it yourself. Not every animation is hostile – a ping toggling between two states is different from a page that shakes itself awake every time you scroll

The mistake is treating these as trade-offs, where turning up clarity means turning down personality. In practice they don't compete with each other nearly as much as people assume.

## Clear doesn't have to mean characterless

This is the line I keep coming back to when I'm designing anything meant to feel calm. It's less a rule and more a check I run on myself: did I remove something because it was genuinely distracting, or did I remove it because _"accessible"_ and _"boring"_ got tangled up in my head somewhere along the way?

Most of the time, when something feels too flat, it's not because it has too much personality— it's because someone reached for uniformity instead of clarity, and called it accessibility. Those aren't the same fix, even though they can look identical on a spec sheet.
