If you've only ever talked to me in person, you probably have a slightly different impression of me than if you've only read my writing. In person, I'm slower to find the right word, more likely to trail off mid-sentence, more likely to say something and immediately want to take it back and phrase it better. In writing, I sound like I know exactly what I mean. That's because I do, eventually. I just need the time to get there.

Speaking is a live process with no edit button. The thought has to form and leave my mouth in roughly the same instant, and if it comes out wrong, it's already out. Writing removes that constraint entirely. I can write a sentence, decide it doesn't say what I mean, delete it, and try again— as many times as I need to, with nobody watching the drafts. What eventually gets sent or posted is the fifth version, not the first, and the fifth version is usually the one that actually says the thing.

There's also just less happening at once. A conversation asks you to track tone, timing, whether the other person's expression means you should keep going or wrap it up, and the actual content of what you're saying, all at the same time, in real time. Writing lets me do these one at a time instead of all at once. I can think first, and shape second, instead of doing both simultaneously and hoping they land.

This is a lot of why I write about things here instead of trying to explain them out loud as they happen. It's not that I don't want to talk about this stuff— it's that writing is where I actually get to sound like myself.
