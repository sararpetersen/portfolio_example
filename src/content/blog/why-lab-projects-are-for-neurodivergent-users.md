If you spend time in [the Lab section](/lab) of this site, you'll notice a pattern I didn't set out to create on purpose: Steady is a daily life tracker for neurodivergent users. AutismeGuiden is an information guide for autistic people and the people around them. Ephemeris is a field journal for tracking energy, focus, and overload. None of these were school assignments or client briefs. They're the projects I chose to build when the choice was entirely mine.

I wrote a short line about this on the Lab page itself, but it deserved more room than a sentence.

### It's not a coincidence, and it's not charity

I want to be precise about what this isn't. It isn't me deciding neurodivergent users are an underserved market and building for them strategically. It's closer to the opposite: when I have a free afternoon and I'm choosing what to prototype, I keep landing on tools for managing energy, overwhelm, and structure— because those are problems I understand from the inside, not problems I researched from the outside.

That distinction matters for the work itself. A lot of software aimed at neurodivergent users is built by people without lived experience of the thing they're designing for, and it shows— in features that perform accessibility rather than practice it, in gamification that assumes everyone is motivated the same way, in language that's clinical where it should be plain. I don't think I automatically avoid every version of that trap just because I have a personal stake in it. But the stake changes what I notice.

### What "designing for calm" actually means in practice

The instinct that runs through Steady, AutismeGuiden, and Ephemeris isn't a specific feature — it's a constraint I keep applying without being asked to: **no performance pressure.** No streaks that guilt you for missing a day. No gamified points that turn a coping tool into another thing to optimize. No cheerful copy pretending a hard day was actually fine.

Concretely, that shows up as things like: letting a tracking app be used inconsistently without punishing the user for it, keeping language plain instead of clinical or falsely upbeat, and building interfaces that don't demand sustained visual attention to parse. None of that is exotic UX theory. It's the accessibility posters I keep on hand distilled into one sentence: don't make the tool one more source of the overwhelm it's supposed to help with.

### Why this belongs in the Lab and not the main case list

The Lab exists for work that's rougher, more personal, and built without a client's constraints softening or sharpening it. That's exactly the right home for these— they're not weaker than my client cases, they're just driven by a different question. My client work asks "what does this business need." These ask "what would have actually helped me, or someone I know, on a bad day." Different question, different kind of honesty in the answer.

### Where this is going

I don't think this pattern is finished. If anything, naming it out loud— on the Lab page, and now here— makes it more likely I keep building this direction on purpose instead of by accident. There's a version of this portfolio a year from now where "designs calm, low-pressure tools for neurodivergent users" isn't a pattern you have to notice by browsing the Lab. It's just what the front page says I do.

It already sort of is. This post is just catching up to it.
